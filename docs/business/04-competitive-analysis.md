# Competitive Analysis

## The Landscape

The fitness app market is massive (~$15B globally) but calisthenics is a specific niche within it. Your competitors fall into three categories:

1. **Big fitness apps** that include some calisthenics
2. **Calisthenics-specific apps** (your direct competitors)
3. **Free alternatives** (YouTube, spreadsheets, notes apps)

## Indirect Competitors

### YouTube + Notes App (The Real Competition)

This is what most beginners actually do:
- Find a routine on YouTube
- Write it in their phone's notes
- Never track progress systematically

**Why people choose this over apps:**
- It's free (truly free, not freemium)
- No commitment
- They don't know what they're missing

**How to beat this:**
- Show them what tracking unlocks (progress charts, PRs, streaks)
- The app needs to be easier than opening Notes — if logging a set takes more than 3 taps, you lose
- Content marketing: "Por que tu app de notas te esta frenando"

### Strong / Hevy / FitNotes (General Gym Trackers)

Popular gym tracking apps that some calisthenics people use.

**Why they're not ideal for calisthenics:**
- Designed for barbell/dumbbell exercises (weight-focused)
- No skill progressions (muscle-up, handstand, planche)
- No periodized bodyweight programs
- No isometric holds tracking

**Your positioning against them:**
- "Hecho para calistenia, no adaptado de gym"
- Proper bodyweight exercise library with progressions
- Isometric timer built-in
- Programs designed for calisthenics periodization

## Your Moat (What's Hard to Copy)

1. **Natively bilingual (ES/EN) with cultural context** — Not just translated UI. Food database works with Latin American products. Exercise names in proper Spanish. WhatsApp sharing (not iMessage/SMS). Full English support opens the app to a global audience without losing its Spanish-first identity.

2. **Complete ecosystem** — Competitors do 1-2 things well. You do workouts + nutrition + cardio + sleep + social + progress + programs + lumbar health. Switching cost is high once someone has 3 months of data.

3. **Offline-first PWA** — Works in areas with bad internet. Critical for LATAM market. Most competitors require constant connection.

4. **AI nutrition in Spanish** — Meal analysis and planning that understands "arepa", "empanada", "tortilla espanola". Not just translated English food databases.

5. **Community features** — Friends, challenges, leaderboards, feed. Network effects mean the app gets more valuable as more people join. Competitors (Thenx, Freeletics) are solo experiences.

6. **Guided onboarding** — Interactive app tours (Driver.js) walk new users through each page. This reduces the "I don't know what to do" drop-off that plagues feature-rich apps. No competitor in this niche offers guided onboarding.

## What You Should NOT Try to Compete On

1. **Brand/influencer status** — You're not Chris Heria. Don't try to be. Compete on product.
2. **AI coaching** — Freeletics has spent millions on this. Your AI advantage is in nutrition, not workout personalization.
3. **Content volume** — Thenx has thousands of professional videos. Your 150+ exercises with embedded YouTube is sufficient, not a differentiator.
4. **App Store ranking** — You're a PWA. You won't rank in app stores without a native wrapper. Focus on direct traffic.
5. **Enterprise/gym market** — Not your audience. Stay B2C, individual athletes.
